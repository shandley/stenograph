This is a great question that gets at a fundamental tension in microbiome research: breadth vs. precision.

The Case for Targeted Assays (qPCR/ddPCR/Hybrid Capture)

For your specific use case, targeted approaches have compelling advantages:

  1. You know what matters - Cervicotypes are defined by a relatively small set of organisms (Lactobacillus species, Gardnerella, Prevotella, Atopobium, Mycoplasma, etc.). You're not doing discovery work.

  2. Absolute quantification - qPCR/ddPCR gives you copies/swab or copies/mL, which is far more biologically meaningful than relative abundance. A "low Lactobacillus" sample could be low in absolute terms or just dominated by something else.

  3. Cost efficiency - You avoid paying to sequence host DNA (often 90%+ of reads in FGT samples).

  4. Sensitivity - Targeted assays can detect low-abundance organisms that get lost in sequencing noise.

  5. Clinical translatability - If you want this to become a diagnostic, qPCR panels are the path forward.

The Case for Keeping Surveys

  1. Unknown unknowns - What if there's a species or strain variant you haven't considered that's driving inflammation?

  2. Community context - Interactions between species matter; a panel misses this.

  3. Strain-level resolution - Metagenomics can distinguish L. crispatus clades with different functional properties.

A Hybrid Strategy Worth Considering

Many groups are moving toward:

  • Targeted qPCR panel for core questions (Lactobacillus species-specific, key BV-associated bacteria, total bacterial load)
  • Shallow metagenomics on a subset for validation and discovery
  • Deep sequencing only on outliers or hypothesis-generating samples

Hybrid Capture Metagenomics: How It Works

The Core Problem

FGT samples are brutal for metagenomics: - 90-99% human DNA in many samples - Shotgun sequencing wastes most reads on host - To get 10M bacterial reads, you might need 100M+ total reads - Cost scales linearly with this inefficiency

The Solution: Capture Enrichment

Hybrid capture uses synthetic oligonucleotide probes (typically 80-120bp) that are complementary to your targets of interest. These probes are biotinylated, allowing you to physically pull down the sequences you want.

2. Key Technical Enhancements to Consider

A. Dual Priming Oligonucleotide (DPO) Technology

This is compelling for your application:

Standard primer:     5'------------------3'
                     (entire sequence primes)

DPO primer:          5'-----[poly-I linker]-----3'
                     18-25nt              6-12nt
                     (stable annealing)   (specificity gate)

Advantages: - Dramatically reduces non-specific amplification - Works across wide annealing temperature range (40-65°C) - Better multiplexing (less primer competition) - The 2024 rpoB paper uses this for direct clinical samples

Application for you: Design DPO versions of your functional gene primers to reduce off-target amplification.

B. Unique Molecular Identifiers (UMIs)

High-accuracy long-read amplicons with UMIs can achieve >99.99% accuracy:

Standard amplicon:
[Primer]---[Target sequence]---[Primer]

UMI amplicon:
[Primer]-[UMI]-[Target sequence]-[UMI]-[Primer]
          ↓
    Random 8-12nt barcode tags each original molecule
          ↓
    Post-sequencing: group reads by UMI → consensus → error correction

What this gives you: - True absolute quantification (count molecules, not reads) - PCR bias correction - Chimera elimination (<0.02% rate) - Strain-level accuracy

Recent advance: Nature Methods 2024 showed that homotrimeric UMI blocks (AAA, TTT, CCC, GGG repeats) provide error-correcting capability.

Recommendation: Add UMIs to your cpn60 and rpoB primers for strain-level work.

January 2025 paper in ISME Communications describes "rDNA-mimics":

Spike-in design:
[Conserved primer binding sites] + [Unique synthetic variable region]
           ↓
Add known copy number to sample before DNA extraction
           ↓
Sequencing reads of spike-in calibrate total microbial load
           ↓
Convert relative → absolute abundance

For your application: - Design cpn60 and rpoB spike-ins with your primer binding sites - Add defined copies before extraction - Enables cross-sample comparison of absolute loads - No separate qPCR needed

Libraries will be sequenced on the Illumina NovaSeq X Plus instrument using the 10B flow cell at the GTAC@MGI sequencing facility at Washington University with the goal of generating approximately 100 million 2x150 reads per sample. Metatranscriptomic functional profiles will be determined using HUMAnN242 with differential abundances tested with edgeR43. Creation of bacterial strain library. Swabs collected at quarterly visits are also frozen in 10% glycerol solution. Upon thawing, isolates will be grown in a variety of media to allow broad propagation of multiple species. Once pure isolates have been obtained, DNA will be extracted and submitted to WGS. Isolates will be identified based on alignment to universal single copy marker genes. As part of this proposal, we will culture, sequence, and store 600 primary bacterial strains. Strains that demonstrate differences in inflammatory potential in the model systems described below will undergo comparative genomic analysis to identify genetic differences that may be the source of altered host-microbe interactions. Our preliminary data (Fig. 4) indicate sub-species structure within isolates from different geographies and support the utility and feasibility of this approach. Characterization of fungal communities in the FGT of women living in sub-Saharan Africa. The FGT is commonly colonized by fungal species, most frequently by species of Candida44. In women from Western countries, approximately 30-50% of women are affected by vulvovaginal candidiasis (VVC) at least once during their lifetime44. While Candida is the dominant fungal genera of the female genital tract, cultivation-independent sequencing efforts have identified previously hidden species diversity45. Fungal colonization of the female genital tract has been associated with changes to resident bacterial communities46 with C. glabrata detection correlated with bacteria from the genus Prevotella. Fungal colonization is also associated with vaginal inflammation with Interleukin (IL)-17 responses thought to play a major role in controlling colonization in mouse models of VVC47. Studies using Gram-staining have associated VVC with increased HIV risk48 and persistent, asymptomatic VVC is associated with elevated cervical Th17-like cells and cytokines49. Understanding fungal colonization patterns in women living in sub-Saharan Africa is required for a full understanding of how the microbiome influences genital inflammation and HIV acquisition risk. ITS2 rRNA amplicon surveys. Fungal community profiles will be characterized using ITS2 rRNA amplicon sequencing. We will amplify a region of ITS2 using the ITS1f-ITS2 primer set50 and sequence to a depth of approximately 50-60,000 paired-end reads using the Illumina MiSeq 2x225 protocol. Reads will be demultiplexed and resolved into sequence variants using DADA233 and taxonomically classified using the UNITE database51. Taxonomic assignments and per sample abundances will be combined with sample data into a phyloseq object35 to facilitate analysis of fungal presence and absence, relative abundance and diversity. Preliminary ITS sequencing from 220 FRESH participants shows high relative abundance of diverse fungal taxa (Fig. 5). Measurement of the vaginal metabolome. The vaginal metabolome is inextricably linked to both microbiota and host function. Metabolome shifts in bacterial vaginosis (BV) have been widely recognized for decades15,16, but detailed understanding of how these metabolic disturbance impact the host are lacking. In preliminary data from 143 FRESH participants, we used ultra-high-performance liquid chromatograph/tandem mass spectrometry (in collaboration with Metabolon, Inc.) and found marked metabolomic differences by vaginal microbiota composition, including alterations of metabolites known to have well-documented adverse host effects at other body sites (Fig. 6). For this proposal, metabolomics data will be generated on all 3600 FRESH CVL samples with Metabolon and then used in Aim 1.3 for feature identification with host inflammatory and HIV acquisition endpoints.

Aim 1.2: Identify specific host genital cellular lineages and microbial sensing pathways that mediate microbiota-associated inflammation and HIV acquisition risk Assessment of inflammation in the FGT. Luminex will be used to assess the level of inflammation in the FGT of women, using a panel of 28 cytokines validated in our prior work (Fig. 1)2,3. We will also measure other soluble mucosal immune markers, such as human defensins, secretory leukocyte peptidase inhibitor (SLPI), lactoferrin, myeloperoxidase and human neutrophil peptides (HNPs), which have been reported to be differentially expressed in the FGT and may represent important readouts of genital inflammation52,53. Quantitation of reproductive hormone levels. Reproductive hormones have been reported to alter both the vaginal microbiome and HIV acquisition risk54 and a study of the temporal dynamics of the vaginal microbiome across the menstrual cycle demonstrated a subset of women have significant variation in the bacterial communities55. These observations suggest a possible linkage between reproductive hormones, the vaginal microbiome, and HIV acquisition risk, and suggest that more detailed studies are needed to understand the relationships between these factors. Systemic progesterone, estradiol, and MPA (medroxyprogesterone acetate, found in DMPA) will be measured by liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS) performed by the Massachusetts General Hospital Clinical Pathology Core. This will allow us to accurately assess the stage in the menstrual cycle sampling was performed for all women56. We will integrate these levels with metadata regarding days since last menstrual period (LMP) and use of hormonal contraception. Characterization of frequency, activation state and co-receptor expression of cervical cells. Cervical cytobrush samples from FRESH participants will be phenotypically characterized by flow cytometry for subset frequency (CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, CD14+ macrophages, CD11c+ and CD123+ DCs, EpCAM+ epithelial cells, and neutrophils), activation state (HLA-DR, CD38, CD69), and HIV co-receptor expression (i.e. CD4 and CCR5) (Fig. 1). These panels have been utilized before in over 300 FRESH cytobrush samples. Use of Seq-Well to perform agnostic high throughput single cell profiling of FGT cellular subsets in women with defined cervicovaginal microbiota. In collaboration with Dr. Alex Shalek at MIT (see LOS), we will utilize a new high throughput microwell technology called Seq-Well to perform single cell RNA sequencing57 (Fig. 7A) from cytobrush samples from the FRESH Study. Seq-Well confines uniquely barcoded mRNA capture beads and single cells in a PDMS microwell array (containing ~ 100,000 wells) sealed with a semi-permeable membrane. Cytobrush, cells are distributed in the plate; the plate is then sealed and frozen for storage. To date, we have successfully captured and generated high quality sequencing libraries from up to 5,000 cells from frozen cervical cytobrushes from participants in the FRESH study from women with both L. crispatus (CT1) and highly diverse (CT4) vaginal microbial communities (Fig. 7B). Using a single device, an average of 40,000-50,000 transcripts per cell can be captured. Once single cell RNAseq has been performed, linear models using the Bioconductor package LIMMA will be utilized for transcriptional profile analyses (i.e. to identify genes which are differentially expressed between specific cell lineages in women with distinct vaginal microbial communities). Differentially expressed genes will be identified based on false discovery rate (FDR) adjusted p value using empirical Bayes moderated tests. The FDR will be controlled using the Benjamini Hochberg procedure. Pathways and interactive networks analysis will be performed on differentially expressed genes using Ingenuity Pathway Analysis, gene set enrichment analysis, and protein interaction databases (STRING58 and FunctionalNet59) to identify candidate host pathways that mediate sensing of inflammatory cervicovaginal microbiota. These candidate pathways will be validated using in vitro and in vivo models in Aim 2. Additionally, exponential random graph models (ERGM) will be used to identify networks of interacting cellular lineages, displayed as an “interactome” of differentially regulated pathways that are predicted to interact between distinct lineages (Fig. 7C). Due to cost, not all cytobrush samples can be analyzed with Seq-Well. We will guide the selection of an informative subset using available microbiome and host data. Specifically, we will embed the non-Seq-Well data into a lower dimensional space and use archetypal analysis to identify representatives for Seq-Well analysis. Since samples with more extreme inflammation (i.e. very high or very low) or microbiome profiles (i.e. L. crispatus dominant or non-dominant) are expected to contain stronger signals, we will adjust the loss function to increase their probability of selection.

Aim 1.3: Clarify disease profiles and candidate mechanisms through integrated multi-omics analysis The data generated by Aims 1.1 and 1.2 have the potential to identify the host-microbiota interactions leading to genital inflammation and HIV acquisition. However, care must be taken to address the challenges of multi-omics data integration. Our approach considers the following challenges: Preprocessing and quality assurance: Careful preprocessing is needed to reduce biases and enhance biological signals in downstream analysis. Characterization of longitudinal changes: Within patient trends in relevant features may provide additional information than their absolute expression levels. Defining disease profiles using integrative dimensionality reduction: The number and high dimensionality of the data modalities complicate the definition of disease profiles. Identifying disease pathways using multi-omics networks: Forming a mechanistic description of disease development requires reasoning about integrated network interactions and causality. Our approach first preprocesses each modality with normalization, batch effect correction, and temporal featurization methods. This boosts true signals and accounts for dependence between samples. Next, we will fit multiblock Partial Least Squares (PLS)60 and iterative Random Forest (iRF)61,62 models to learn integrated disease profiles and interaction networks. For longitudinal samples we will define summary variables for each feature to quantify the longitudinal variability and trends for each participant. Finally, we will share intermediate data and modeling artifacts through web and code interfaces, ensuring that our analysis effort is transparent and can be reused by the community. Our approach will be applied to integrate and interrogate data generated in Aim 1.1 and Aim 1.2 with the goal of identifying microbes, microbial products, host cell and host-responses that define genital inflammation and HIV acquisition. These features will serve as substrate for Aim 2. Preliminary Data: We previously completed an integrative, multi-omics analysis to evaluate a probiotic intervention, Lactin-V, which is intended to reduce the rate of recurrent BV (rBV) after antibiotic treatment63. We quantified feature and block importance from host demographic, behavioral, immunological, and microbiome data using PLS (Fig. 8). Since all blocks were included in the model simultaneously, the effect of any one block can be interpreted as conditional on all others being held fixed. We found that past microbiota composition and perturbations (e.g., vaginal hygiene practices) of the vaginal environment are more predictive of treatment success than all demographic or sexual behavioral characteristics. Further, a Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA)64,65 found a strong association between microbiome subcommunity composition and host cytokine activity (Fig. 9), with the top two canonical dimensions having correlations of 0.7 and 0.32. Research Design: Protocols for preprocessing and quality assurance. Distinguishing technical from biological variation is a central difficulty in multi-omics analysis, and careful data preparation is vital for valid inference. To facilitate this analysis, we will sequence negative control samples across modalities. Negative controls represent background variation due to sample preparation and assay noise. If batch effects are detected, these negative controls can be input to either CellAnova66 or RUV467,68 for correction, depending on whether the assay is sequencing-based. Further, we must be mindful of missing data, especially for metabolomics, which will be processed with a peak-detection pipeline. Different mechanisms can lead to missingness – for example, falling below the lower limit of quantification or peak-detection failure during alignment – and we will impute values in a way that respects these differences69. We will apply variance stabilizing transformations to 16S/ITS, metagenomic, metatranscriptomics, cytokine, cellular phenotype, and scRNAseq data to improve the statistical efficiency of downstream analysis. We will compute quality metrics on these normalized data using Scater70 and will exclude low-quality outliers from analysis. These sample-level checks will be coupled with cohort-level visualization of sampling times to flag inconsistencies. Finally, beyond sample processing, errors can result from inaccurate sample tracking. We will link metadata to digital metadata files that are curated and QC’d, mitigating the risks associated with manual data entry and transfer. Characterizing longitudinal changes. Each participant of the FRESH study provided samples approximately every three months for three time points. Because of the low sampling frequency relative to the typical dynamics of microbiota composition in individuals (previous studies have shown that the microbiota composition can drastically change over a few days or weeks71) dynamical models of the vaginal ecosystems are unlikely to accurately describe temporal changes between visits. Instead, we will define summary variables for each feature to quantify the longitudinal variability and trends for each participant. Specifically, each feature will be summarized for each participant by its mean value and trend. Trends will be computed as the slope of the line regressed over the available time points and considered missing for participants with a single time point. If we observe many features with large variances but small trends (e.g., a sequence of high-low-high values), we will include the variance in the summary variables. Since this increases the number of features (but not the number of samples), we would then use regularization in downstream analyses to impose sparsity in the profiles associated with genital inflammation or HIV acquisition (see below). Defining disease profiles using integrative dimensionality reduction. Given the preprocessed data, we will generate an integrated, exploratory overview of disease-associated features using PLS or sparse PLS (sPLS). PLS is a foundational method in multi-omics analysis72-74 and has been applied to a variety of settings, including childhood asthma75, diabetes76, and COVID-1977. In our context, it will produce latent profiles of genital inflammation and HIV acquisition across modalities. Specifically, we will apply the mixOmics60 R package with longitudinally summarized 16S/ITS, metagenomic, metatranscriptomic, cytokine, cellular phenotypic, metabolomic, scRNAseq, demographic, and behavioral data as input blocks and inflammation and HIV infection status as the response variables. The number of factors will be tuned by cross-validation. This supervised dimensionality reduction mirrors the approach used on the preliminary data but with inflammation and HIV status. By including demographic and behavioral features as inputs, we will control for them, helping illuminate microbiological and immunological effects beyond superficial host differences. The model will output latent profiles ─ patterns of features that are predictive of the inflammatory and infection response variables ─ for each input block. Features with strong associations (i.e., factor loadings with large magnitudes) may play a direct role in disease development and are natural candidates for follow up studies in Aim 2. Of particular interest are the latent profiles associated with immunological activity. From prior work, we expect these profiles to be highly predictive of HIV infection risk. If these findings are replicated, the resulting profiles will be reliable proxies for infection risk that could be used during model-system experiments. Further, the PLS analysis will return importance scores for each modality. These scores will prioritize relevant disease-associated features, since large effects tend to be proximate to underlying disease mechanisms. Identifying disease pathways using multi-omics networks. The PLS analysis provides an exploratory view of disease associations. However, a more precise description of cellular interactions is necessary to guide experimental investigation. Multi-omics interaction networks, which are analogs to gene expression networks that tie modalities together, can reveal specific pathways of disease development. To build these networks, we will apply iRF61,62, a random forest-based interaction discovery method. Our input and response will be formatted as in the PLS; however, the output of the iRF model are feature-level interactions that are predictive of either inflammatory or HIV state. For example, a bacterial taxon (16S data) may increase the levels of an immune cell type (scRNAseq data or cell phenotyping) that is targeted by HIV, ultimately resulting in increased disease risk. This mechanism would systematically elevate the expression of that pair of taxonomic and immunological features within HIV-infected women. The feature pair defines a predictive association rule and hence can be learned by iRF. We can subsequently build a general interaction network by combining association rules from across the model. One limitation of this analysis is that the learned iRF interactions are associational, not causal. In the example above, an interaction between a bacterial taxon and an immune cell type may be visible, but we would not be able to conclude that the taxon leads to alterations in immune cell frequency. In general, causal discovery is a challenging statistical problem. However, the diversity of our multi-omics data allows us to apply a causal invariance analysis to the network, helping to screen away non-causal effects. Specifically, for each node in the undirected association network, we will apply stabilized regression78 with that node as the response, direct neighbors as candidate causal features, and all other nodes as the background environment. Stabilized regression is built on the observation that upstream causal nodes in a biological pathway maintain consistent associations even in shifting environments, while noncausal, downstream associations tend to be sensitive to environmental perturbation. By iterating this procedure across neighborhoods in the estimated network, we can identify and prioritize stable directed edges (i.e. pointing at specific microbial taxa or immune cells) for experimental follow-up. Enhancing Transparency and Reproducibility. We will prepare data, model outputs, and code notebooks so that they can become a resource for the microbiome multi-omics research community. We will host raw data on a permanent URL through our library system, except for sequencing reads, which we will deposit in the Sequence Read Archive. Preprocessed data will be shared as an R Bioconductor ExperimentData package and in a language-agnostic form through Zenodo79,80.Estimated model parameters, including the learned PLS profiles and iRF network, will be hosted publicly as RData objects and CSV files. A helper R package will encapsulate the low-level operations of our analysis. These functions will be called in compiled Rmarkdown reports that discuss the analysis, allow others to reproduce it, and support adaptation to new data81. These notebooks will be hosted on GitHub, and we will document the repository to clarify the workflow and engage the community. Power analysis for Aim 1. To ensure sufficient power to identify meaningful multi-omic signatures given the collected number of samples, we relied on simulations. We generated synthetic data associated with each modality, introduced hypothetical HIV-associated shifts, and measured recovery of the true effect. We used the sparseDOSSA282 and ZINB-WaVE83 models to simulate baseline vaginal 16S rRNA and metagenomic sequencing data. Though ZINB-WaVE was designed for scRNAseq data, it uses a low-rank negative binomial factorization that has been competitive in metagenomics benchmarks84. We simulated metabolomics and behavioral data from gamma and categorical distributions, respectively. We then defined a sparse regression model where 10% of features from each modality relate to the response, fixed the signal-to-noise ratio to 3, and calculated error rates across sample sizes. When enforcing a False Discovery Rate control level of 0.15, a sample with 1200 subjects has an average power of 88.7% across assays (75% power for the simulated survey, 90% for the 16S, 90% for the metagenomic, and 100% for the metabolomic data). In contrast, applying the analysis with a sample of size 600 had low average power (55.0%), and a larger sample size of 1800 yielded diminishing returns (93.8% average power). Hence, the intended sample size balances efficacy with economy. Potential pitfalls and alternative approaches for Aim 1. Multi-omics analysis can be sensitive to specific preprocessing hyperparameters, like the choice of variance stabilization function or the selection of confounding variables during batch effect correction. Though the large sample size of the proposed study partially mitigates these risks, we must still guard against potential spurious associations that preprocessing can introduce. To this end, we will measure the concordance of selected candidate features/interactions on different data splits when using different hyperparameter choices. Our final analysis will only incorporate hyperparameter selections with high between-split concordance. A second potential pitfall is that imbalance between the number of FRESH participants who do and do not acquire HIV may degrade model performances. Since we will use supervised methods to predict the binary variable flagging HIV acquisition, we may address this class imbalance by increasing the weight of samples (participants) from the minority class, or, equivalently, by altering the loss function. Another potential pitfall is computational rather than statistical. If the learned interaction network is dense with many potential connections, then stabilizing regression can become computationally challenging, even after parallelization across the high-performance computer cluster used by our team. In this case, we will restrict the causal invariance analysis to the subset of edges with the strongest associations, sacrificing the completeness of the causal characterization for improved computational efficiency.

Aim 2: Investigate candidate pathways of microbiota-mediated genital inflammation and HIV acquisition risk using novel in vitro and in vivo models A significant technical problem in the field is the lack of a single accepted model for the human FGT20, particularly one that incorporates microbiota, host cells, and HIV infection. We have developed three model systems that we will use to test candidate bacterial strains and metabolites chosen based upon our preliminary work as well as from features identified in Aim 1: 1) a 3D vagina-on-a-chip (“V-Chip”), 2) vaginal organoids, and 3) humanized mice. These complementary systems will allow us to characterize mechanistic relationships between candidate mediators and readouts of host inflammation and/or HIV acquisition. To begin with, we will define a set of candidate mediators based upon our prior work and published literature while work described in Aim 1 occurs in parallel to define additional candidate features.

Candidate mediators of genital inflammation and HIV acquisition risk to test in model systems. Our preliminary results have already identified several candidate bacterial species, metabolites, and host inflammatory pathways to test in model systems. This includes biogenic amines, such as the metabolites putrescine and cadaverine, which are highly enriched in the vagina of women with CT3 and CT4 microbiota and have been described as having pro-viral effects at other body sites (Fig. 10A). Putrescine has small molecule synthesis inhibitors that can also be tested using our model systems. In addition, RNA transcriptional analysis of cervical antigen presenting cells (APCs) demonstrated strong upregulation of bacterial lipopolysaccharide (LPS) signaling via toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) pathways (Fig. 10B). This coincides with significant increases in LPS in CT3 and CT4 communities based upon bacterial metagenomic analysis. Regarding candidate vaginal bacterial species that may mediate increased HIV acquisition, we previously identified Prevotella melaninogenica, Veillonella montpellierensis, Mycoplasma, Sneathia sanguinegens, and two strains of Prevotella bivia as being associated with both increased genital inflammation and HIV acquisition in FRESH (Fig. 10C). These candidate metabolites, host pathways, and bacterial species will be used initially in model systems to explore mechanistic relationships to genital inflammation and HIV infection. 3D organ-on-a-chip vaginal epithelial model (V-Chip). One of the challenges in the vaginal microbiome field is that there has not been a robust way to study how living human vaginal epithelium interacts directly with living microbes because conventional co-cultures lead to bacterial overgrowth and cell death85-88. In addition, they fail to recapitulate physiologically relevant tissue-tissue interfaces and critical physical cues89. Organ-on-a-chip models provide more robust options for in vitro testing of complex interactions with multifaceted microbial communities27,89,90. The V-Chip is a novel, 3D microfluidic organ-on-a-chip model of the vaginal epithelium, which supports testing of hypotheses regarding microbe-host interactions27. Developed in collaboration with Dr. Don Ingber, the V-Chip is created by co-culturing primary human vaginal epithelium on the top surface of an extracellular matrix-coated porous membrane within the top channel of a two-channel microfluidic chip with primary human uterine fibroblasts on the lower surface of the same membrane in the bottom parallel channel to recreate the vaginal epithelial-stromal interface (Fig. 11A-C). The V-Chip model allows integration of microbial and host characteristics, and the microfluidic platform allows for long-term co-culture, introduction of varying exposures, and frequent sampling of the community composition and metabolic output of the system. When co-cultured with L. crispatus strain consortia, the V-Chips maintain vaginal epithelial cell viability, low pH, and the bacterial production of D-lactate, while downregulating proinflammatory cytokines, all of which recapitulate findings in women (Fig. 11D-F). V-Chips are commercially available from Enumeral (see LOS from Dr. Don Ingber) and will be used to test candidate microbial strains, host pathways, and metabolites that are identifed from Aim 1 and/or our preliminary findings. V-Chips will be evaluated for bacterial composition (for those colonized with bacterial strains and/or consortia), metabolites production (metabolomics), and host response (immunological markers and transcriptional analysis). Vaginal organoids with autologous tissue resident T cells. To complement the V-Chip model, we will use vaginal organoids with autologous tissue resident T cells derived from excess hysterectomy tissue (Fig. 12). These organoids can be expanded in vitro and co-cultured with bacteria (for short term culture <24 hours), metabolites, and small molecule inhibitors. We will also develop the model so that we can infect the tissue resident CD4+ T cells with HIV and measure the impact of candidate features on HIV viral replication at low multiplicity of infection (MOI) using HIV p24 ELISA over the first 10 days after infection. Assessment of candidate features in a humanized mouse model of intravaginal HIV acquisition. We will utilize the humanized mouse model, which is an established model for vaginal HIV infection91-93, to assess the impact of candidate features on genital inflammation and HIV susceptibility. We have shown that vaginal inoculation of these mice with P. bivia, but not L. crispatus, induces a similar genital inflammatory cytokine response and increases in FGT CD4+ T cell as observed in women in FRESH (Fig. 13A, B)2. Additionally, vaginal inoculation with P. bivia also resulted in increased HIV acquisition with repeated low dose vaginal HIV challenge (Fig. 13C, D). To test candidate features, mice will be intravaginally inoculated each week on days 0 and 2 with microbes (bacterial and/or fungal strains, alone or as consortia), metabolites, or other molecules of interest, including potential inflammation inhibitors. On day 3, mice will undergo vaginal low dose HIV challenge with 2500 TCID50/mL JRCSF; the challenge dose is chosen such that 10-15% of saline inoculated control mice are infected with each challenge. The series of weekly vaginal inoculation and HIV challenge will be repeated for 10 weeks. We will compare HIV infection rates in treated mice vs saline inoculated control mice (12 mice per group). Blood viral load (VL) will be assessed weekly and time to infection compared between mice receiving different vaginal inocula and treatments. Animals will be sacrificed once HIV is detected or after 10 weeks, when CVL, blood, and the FGT tissue will be collected. Half of the FGT will be used for flow cytometry the same day, while the other half will be digested and loaded on Seq-Well for scRNAseq. Flow cytometry will be performed to assess cellular markers of genital inflammation as described above. Secondarily, CVL fluid and plasma (control) will be used to examine local and systemic cytokines and chemokines using Luminex. CVL fluid will further be used to assess the presence of live bacteria and the bacterial composition.
Statistical analysis for Aim 2: Measures of inflammation and pathway activation will be compared in each model system between different conditions using t-test, ANOVA, or nonparametric methods as appropriate. scRNAseq results will be analyzed as described in Aim 1.2 and will be compared to findings generated from cervical samples from FRESH participants. For the organoid experiments we will conduct at least 3 experiments per condition, each with technical replicates. In preliminary experiments, our standard deviation for IL-8 values across donors and bacterial treatments is 0.35 log10, thus with 4 donors per comparison we would have at least 80% power to detect a 1.5 log10 difference between conditions. For mouse experiments with at least 4 mice per group in each of 3 experiments (i.e., 12 mice per group), analyzed using a two-sided Wilcoxon rank sum test (level α=0.05) stratified by experiment, there would be at least 80% power to detect a true standardized difference (stdiff) of 1.23 in numbers of activated CD4+ T cells between conditions, which is smaller than the observed stdiff of 1.48 for CD4+ T cells seen in our prior work2. We will compare p24 quantity between conditions in organoids and mice using student’s t-test. For mouse experiments, the number of mice suggested is based on a 10-15% infection rate with the indicated dose of virus, assuming that some conditions would increase the infection rate 3-fold and inhibition would decrease that by at least half, to 45%. We will test the proportion of infected mice between treatment conditions using a one-sided Fisher exact test with a cut-off of 0.05. Potential pitfalls and alternative approaches for Aim 2. There are caveats to the use of any model system. However, we propose comparing results directly to those obtained from women in FRESH to make sure that the models recapitulate important observations identified in Aim 1. Discrepancies will be used to inform in vitro model improvements, and only after its fidelity can be guaranteed will we move forward with mechanistic work in the model. UMI Analysis: Pros and Cons for Your Application

What UMIs Actually Give You

Without UMI:
  Original molecules → PCR amplification → Sequencing
                            ↓
  10 copies of molecule A → 1000 reads (100x amplification)
  10 copies of molecule B → 100 reads (10x amplification)
                            ↓
  Relative abundance: 91% A, 9% B (WRONG - should be 50/50)

With UMI:
  Original molecules → Tag with unique barcode → PCR → Sequencing
                            ↓
  Molecule A-UMI1 → 100 reads → collapse to 1
  Molecule A-UMI2 → 150 reads → collapse to 1
  ...
  Molecule A-UMI10 → 80 reads → collapse to 1
  Molecule B-UMI1 → 10 reads → collapse to 1
  ...
  Molecule B-UMI10 → 12 reads → collapse to 1
                            ↓
  Count UMIs: 10 for A, 10 for B = 50/50 (CORRECT)

Pros

Benefit Relevance to your project Impact
True molecule counting Absolute quantification without qPCR High - you have spike-ins, UMIs make them work better
PCR bias correction GC-rich/poor sequences amplify differently High - Gardnerella and Lactobacillus have different GC content
Chimera elimination PCR chimeras are major problem in amplicon studies High - your multi-gene approach compounds this risk
Strain deconvolution Distinguish strain mixtures in same sample Very high - your Fig 3 shows strain structure matters
Error correction Consensus from multiple reads of same molecule Medium - Illumina already high quality
Low-template accuracy Critical when few molecules present Medium - FGT generally high biomass

Cons

Drawback Mitigation Severity
Increased sequencing depth needed Need ~10-25x coverage per UMI for consensus Moderate - NovaSeq handles this
More expensive primers ~$50-100 more per primer pair Low - one-time cost
Bioinformatics complexity Need UMI processing pipeline Moderate - tools exist (DADA2-UMI, AmpUMI)
Reduced effective read depth Collapse reads → fewer data points Moderate - offset by accuracy
Requires higher input DNA Need enough molecules for UMI diversity Low - FGT samples generally good
Index hopping risk on NovaSeq UMIs can help detect this Actually a pro
