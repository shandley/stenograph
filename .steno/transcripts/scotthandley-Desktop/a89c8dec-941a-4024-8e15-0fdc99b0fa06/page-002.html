Methods to distinguish sgRNA from genomic RNA:

  1. Leader-junction PCR: sgRNAs have a characteristic structure - they all contain the 5' leader sequence (~70nt) fused directly to the body of each gene. You can design primers that span this leader-body junction to specifically amplify sgRNAs. One primer in the leader, one in the gene body. This is the most direct way to detect sgRNAs.

  2. Strand-specific RT-PCR: sgRNAs are positive-sense only (they're mRNAs). During active replication, you'd also have negative-sense RNA intermediates. Detecting negative-sense RNA would strongly indicate active replication.

  3. Northern blot: Can separate RNAs by size and probe for specific genes - you'd see different sized bands for genomic vs subgenomic.

  4. Nanopore/long-read sequencing: Could directly sequence full-length RNAs and identify sgRNA junctions.

  5. 5' RACE or similar: To identify the 5' ends of the transcripts.

  6. Re-analysis of existing data: Look for leader-body junction reads in the existing sequencing data, though with ARTIC amplicons this may be limited.

  7. RNAscope with different probes: Use probes targeting 3' genes (N, M) vs Spike to see if there's differential signal, which might indicate sgRNA predominance.

The most practical and commonly used method is leader-junction RT-PCR. This has been published and used to detect sgRNAs in clinical samples.
